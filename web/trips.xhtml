1. Dumbéa is situated north of the capital Nouméa and is the 3rd largest town in New Caledonia. It is named after the “Doumbéa river” which services Noumea city on one hand, and irrigates a vast vegetable/fruit-growing plain on the other, before flowing into the sea. Dumbéa is also known for its unique primary tropical forest. This visit seeks to present the challenges faced by the Dumbéa river basin in terms of concurrently managing water resources, natural disaster risk, and environmental protection. Discussion will focus on: (1) water resource management in the water basin, (2) the 2005 bush fires, their impact and lessons learnt for enhanced fire management (including fire prevention and environmental restoration efforts), (3) challenges and threats to the area, and (4) efforts to protect this unique environment as a ‘provincial nature park.’

2. Yaté, located in the Southern tip of the main island, is home to New Caledonia’s most striking natural sites. It is also rich in nickel which is exploited through a large mining operation since 2009. This visit will focus on the efforts of the municipality to balance industrial growth with the preservation of its environmental and cultural heritage. Focus areas: (1) disaster risk management (Yaté is prone to flooding, tsunami and industrial and chemical pollution, with impact on water resources), (2) water resource management, (3) land use planning, (4) environmental protection.

3. Known for its surfing waves and in close proximity to the lagoon (a UNESCO world heritage site), Bourail is a rural town 160 km north of the capital Nouméa. Since 2009, Bourail has a vibrant water management committee, bringing together water stakeholders and users to enhance the management of water resources. This visit will present: (1) the objectives of the committee, (2) its growth and structure, (3) key actions undertaken such as the protection of water catchment areas, inventory of resources, assessment of water needs and usage, and types and amounts of wastewater, (3) the particular challenges in terms of the proximity of the site to the lagoon, a UNESCO world heritage site.

4. Water safety planning is being implemented throughout New Caledonia as an approach to reduce the risk of contamination of drinking water. An increasing numbers of municipalities, such as Bourail, are developing water safety plans and putting in place preventive and corrective measures to ensure the safety of drinking water (both in normal times and disaster situations). This risk management approach applied to water safety will be presented both from a national and a municipality perspective. Field visits will enable participants to witness measures taken on-the-ground and learn about management arrangements and solutions rooted in the New Caledonia context.
